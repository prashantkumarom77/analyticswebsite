Handling Corporate Actions in Long-Term Equities

Oct 12, 2025 Data Engineering Team 6 min read

When building quantitative models on historical cash market data, one of the most common pitfalls developers face is the failure to properly account for corporate actions. Unadjusted data introduces artificial price drops that can trigger false stop-losses and ruin backtest integrity.

1. The Danger of Unadjusted Data

Consider a 2:1 stock split. If a stock closes at ₹1,000 on Monday and opens at ₹500 on Tuesday post-split, an unadjusted dataset records a massive 50% loss. An algorithmic momentum strategy reading this raw data will instantly execute a sell order, misinterpreting a routine corporate action as a catastrophic market event.

2. Normalizing for Splits and Bonuses

To maintain a continuous, accurate price history, historical prices prior to the ex-date must be mathematically adjusted. At LLUBRAEB Analytics, our historical databases automatically apply an adjustment factor to all Open, High, Low, and Close (OHLC) prices, ensuring smooth data continuity for long-term moving averages and oscillators.

"A strategy is only as robust as the data it was trained on. Garbage in, execution errors out."

— Lead Quant, LLUBRAEB

3. The Complexity of Dividends

While splits are straightforward, dividends require a choice. Regular cash dividends are often ignored in standard price adjustments unless they are unusually large (special dividends). However, for Total Return strategies, we provide specialized endpoints that factor in dividend reinvestment, painting a true picture of total yield over a 10-year span.

4. Open Interest (OI) and Derivatives

Corporate actions don't just affect cash markets; they shift option strike prices and lot sizes overnight. When backtesting F&O data spanning a corporate action, our datasets seamlessly link the pre-action strikes to their post-action adjusted equivalents, allowing for uninterrupted Greek calculations.

5. Why Pure Data Matters

Scrubbing this data internally requires massive engineering overhead. By utilizing an institutional-grade API that delivers pre-adjusted, pristine historical dumps, your engineering team can spend their time optimizing execution algorithms rather than cleaning erroneous ticks.
